# Feeding and Sleep for Babies (4-6 Months)

As your baby reaches 4 to 6 months, their sleep patterns may start to become a little more predictable, with some babies beginning to have longer stretches of sleep at night, potentially up to 6 hours by 6 months. However, it's still very common for babies in this age group to wake at least once, and often multiple times, for night feeds. Their small tummies still require regular nourishment, and these feeds are important for their continued growth and development.

You might hear suggestions that introducing formula or solids will help your baby sleep longer, but research doesn't support this. Breastfeeding mothers often report getting more sleep than those who formula-feed, partly because the hormones in breastmilk can help both mum and baby drift back to sleep. Starting solids before 6 months is unlikely to improve sleep and may even cause tummy upset.

Feeding to sleep remains a natural and effective settling tool for many families. It provides comfort and satisfies hunger, helping your baby relax into sleep. If your baby is easily distracted during daytime feeds, they might "reverse cycle," meaning they make up for missed feeds at night.

## Key Points
- Night feeds are still common and often necessary for babies aged 4-6 months.
- There is no evidence that formula or early solids will make your baby sleep longer.
- Breastfeeding to sleep is a natural and comforting way for babies to settle.
- Breastfed babies may need night feeds to maintain milk supply.
- Night weaning for formula-fed babies can be considered from 6 months, but it's generally not recommended for breastfed babies before 12 months.

## What to Try
- **Continue responsive feeding:** Follow your baby's hunger cues, ensuring they get enough feeds during the day.
- **Offer extra daytime feeds:** If your baby is distractible during the day, offering more frequent feeds when they are alert can help reduce the need for night feeds.
- **Consider a 'dream feed':** A late-night top-up feed between 10 pm and midnight, before you go to bed, might help some babies sleep a bit longer.
- **Keep night interactions minimal:** When your baby wakes for a feed, keep lights dim and interactions quiet to reinforce that night-time is for sleep.
- **Discuss night weaning (formula-fed, 6m+):** If your baby is formula-fed and around 6 months old, and you feel they are ready, you can discuss phasing out night feeds with your child health nurse.

## When to Seek Help
- If your baby is not gaining weight adequately or you have concerns about their feeding patterns.
- If your baby is consistently very sleepy or difficult to rouse for feeds.
- If you are feeling overwhelmed, exhausted, or experiencing symptoms of low mood or anxiety; reach out to your GP or child and family health nurse for support.
