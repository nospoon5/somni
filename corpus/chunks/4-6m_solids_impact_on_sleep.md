# Solids and Sudden Night Waking

If a 4-6 month old starts solids and sleep gets worse, the first question is not "Did solids break sleep forever?" It is "What changed around feeds, timing, or tummy comfort this week?"

Starting solids does **not** reliably make babies sleep through. Milk remains the main nutrition source at this age, and many babies sleep worse for a few days when solids are introduced because the routine changed, the food was offered too late, or their gut is adjusting.

## The Decision Tree

### If the baby has only just started solids

The rough night is often about **novelty, timing, or too much too soon**, not a sign they now need more solids.

Common patterns:

- solids were offered close to bedtime
- a large serve replaced a milk feed
- several new foods were introduced quickly
- the baby became gassy, constipated, or unsettled after the new food

### If the parent is hoping solids will stop hunger wakes

Do not treat solids as the first-line fix for night waking. At 4-6 months, a baby who still wakes overnight may still need milk, may be in a developmental sleep shift, or may simply be overtired. More puree is not a guaranteed sleep fix.

### If the baby seems uncomfortable after solids

Think tummy, not training:

- firmer stools or straining
- obvious wind after the evening meal
- upset after a brand-new food
- vomiting, rash, diarrhoea, or blood/mucus, which needs medical review

## What To Do Tonight

- **Keep milk first.** If the baby normally has a milk feed before bed, do not replace it with solids.
- **Do not push extra solids at dinner to "fill them up".** That usually backfires.
- **If solids were offered late today and bedtime was messy, go back to the usual milk-and-bed routine tonight.**
- **If a new food clearly lined up with the rough night, simplify tomorrow.** Offer one familiar food earlier in the day after milk, not at the end of the day when it is hard to tell whether the issue is food or overtiredness.

## A Better Setup For Tomorrow

- Offer solids earlier in the day while you are still learning what suits their tummy
- Keep portions small at the start
- Introduce one new food at a time
- Protect daytime milk feeds so calories are not accidentally shifted the wrong way

## What To Stop Doing

- Do not add solids because the baby had one bad night
- Do not assume every wake after starting solids means hunger
- Do not keep trialling multiple new foods at dinner
- Do not add cereal to bottles or improvise "sleepier" feeds

## When To Seek Help

- vomiting, rash, wheezing, diarrhoea, blood or mucus in stools
- ongoing constipation or obvious pain after feeds
- poor milk intake, poor weight gain, or a baby who seems unwell rather than just unsettled
