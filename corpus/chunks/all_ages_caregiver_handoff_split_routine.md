# Caregiver Handoff and Split-Routine Scenarios

Babies and toddlers are often cared for by multiple people: parents, grandparents, nannies, or daycare educators. It is common for each caregiver to have a different way of settling the child. The good news is that babies are very context-dependent and perfectly capable of learning that different people have different sleep rules.

## What is Happening

Your child associates specific cues and routines with specific people. If Grandma rocks them to sleep, they expect rocking from Grandma. If you put them down awake, they expect that from you. Friction only happens when one caregiver tries to abruptly change what the child expects *from them*, or when a caregiver handles a night wake inconsistently.

## What to Do Tonight (or Today)

- **Define the primary night routine:** The person doing the bedtime routine and managing night wakes must be consistent. This is the anchor for the child's sleep. Ensure the primary caregiver uses the agreed-upon settling method (e.g., placing them down awake).
- **Manage the handoff smoothly:** If Caregiver A is frustrated, it is okay for Caregiver B to tag in. However, Caregiver B should try to continue the same settling method rather than immediately resorting to a higher-intervention method (like feeding to sleep) just to "get it done", otherwise the child learns to stall for Caregiver B.
- **Communicate the non-negotiables:** Tell secondary caregivers (grandparents, nannies) the core rules: e.g., "safe sleeping is non-negotiable", "do not let them nap past 3pm", or "try to put them down awake first".

## What Compromise is Acceptable

- **Day vs Night variations:** It is perfectly acceptable for a nanny or grandparent to use a higher-intervention method (like rocking or pram-walking) for a day nap if it ensures the child gets enough daytime sleep. The priority during the day is sleep pressure management so that the night routine remains intact.
- **Different strokes for different folks:** Do not stress if your partner settles the baby differently than you do, as long as it works for them and doesn't create new entrenched night-waking habits.

## When to Review the Plan Again

- Review after 3-4 days if night sleep begins to fragment or if bedtime battles significantly increase. If this happens, you may need to ask secondary caregivers to align more closely with your primary settling method to reduce the child's confusion.
