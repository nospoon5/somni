# Active Sleep, Grunting, and the Startle Reflex

Watching a newborn sleep can be alarming if you don't know what's normal. Grunting, twitching, grimacing, moaning, and random arm flings are all — often — completely normal. Understanding the difference between normal active sleep and a sign of distress will save you many panicked midnight interventions.

## What Is Active Sleep?

Newborns spend approximately **50% of their sleep time in active sleep** (also called REM or "rapid eye movement" sleep). This is far more than adults (who spend about 20–25% in REM). Active sleep is when the brain consolidates learning and supports neurological development — it is critically important.

During active sleep, you may observe:
- Eyelids fluttering or slightly open (eyes moving under the lids)
- Facial grimacing, smiling, or frowning
- Grunting or making vocalisations
- Limb twitching or jerking
- Irregular breathing rhythm
- The body moving rhythmically but not waking

This is all **normal active sleep**. If their eyes are closed and they settle without intervention, **do not pick them up.**

## The Startle (Moro) Reflex

The Moro reflex is a primitive reflex present from birth. When a newborn senses a sudden change in position, a loud noise, or a feeling of falling, their arms fly out wide and then pull back in — often waking them from a light sleep.

This reflex typically diminishes between 3–6 months as the nervous system matures.

**Swaddling** is the most effective intervention. A snug swaddle suppresses the arm-flinging response and helps newborns stay asleep through the startle. Once rolling begins (usually 3–4 months), discontinue swaddling immediately.

## How to Tell Normal Active Sleep from Distress

| Sign | Normal active sleep | Possible concern |
|------|---------------------|------------------|
| Eyes | Closed, fluttering | Prolonged wide-open with blank stare |
| Breathing | Irregular but resumes normally | Stops for 20+ seconds, colour change |
| Movement | Twitching, brief | Repetitive rhythmic jerking of the same limb |
| Response | Settles without intervention | Escalates, cannot be calmed |
| Colour | Normal skin tone | Blue around lips, or pale/grey |

If you ever observe blue discolouration around the mouth, a breath-holding episode lasting more than 20 seconds, or your baby appearing limp or unresponsive — **call 000 immediately.**

## The "10-Minute Wait" for Newborns

Many parents wake a soundly sleeping baby during active sleep by picking them up the moment they grunt or twitch. The baby then struggles to resettle. If your newborn is making noises but their eyes are closed, wait 60–90 seconds before responding. They are often cycling through a brief active sleep phase and will return to quiet sleep on their own.

## Key Points

- Active sleep looks alarming but is developmentally normal and essential
- Grunting, twitching, and grimacing with closed eyes = do not intervene
- Swaddling significantly reduces the Moro reflex waking
- If any sign of distress is present AND the baby is awake, respond promptly

## When to Seek Help

- Any breath-holding of 20+ seconds, blueness around the lips, or limpness: call 000 immediately
- Repetitive rhythmic jerking of a single limb (not the general Moro flail): speak to your GP the same day — this can occasionally indicate a neurological event
- If you are consistently unable to tell whether your baby is in distress or not, and the uncertainty is causing you severe anxiety, speak to your GP or child health nurse
