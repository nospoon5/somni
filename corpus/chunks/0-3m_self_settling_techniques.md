# Self-Settling for Newborns (0-3 Months)

Welcome to the world of newborn sleep! It's a unique and often unpredictable journey. In these early months, your baby is learning so much, and their sleep patterns are still developing. Newborns typically sleep between 14-17 hours a day, but in short bursts of 2-4 hours, waking frequently for feeds and comfort. Their internal body clock, or circadian rhythm, is just starting to figure out the difference between day and night.

At this stage, the focus isn't on "sleep training" in the traditional sense, but rather on "responsive settling." This means tuning into your baby's cues and providing the comfort and reassurance they need to feel safe and secure. You can't spoil a newborn with too much love and cuddles; responding to their needs helps build trust and a strong bond. Methods like "cry it out" are generally not recommended for this age group.

## Key Points
- Newborns have disorganised sleep patterns and short sleep cycles (around 40-50 minutes).
- They need frequent feeds and comfort, both day and night.
- Responding to your baby's cries and offering comfort is crucial for their development and sense of security.
- "Cry it out" or "controlled crying" methods are not suitable for babies under 3-4 months.

## What to Try
- **Watch for Tired Signs:** Look for cues like yawning, frowning, jerky movements, fussiness, or rubbing eyes. Respond promptly to these to help your baby settle before they become overtired.
- **Offer Comfort:** Gentle holding, rocking, patting, soft singing, or quiet "shush" sounds can help your baby relax and drift off to sleep.
- **Ensure Needs are Met:** Before settling, make sure your baby has a clean nappy and has been well-fed. A full tummy and a dry bottom make settling much easier.
- **Create a Calm Environment:** Dim the lights and keep the room quiet to signal that it's time for rest.
- **Consider Wrapping:** Many newborns benefit from being wrapped in a lightweight cotton or muslin cloth, as it can help reduce their startle reflex. Ensure their arms are free once they show signs of rolling (around 3-4 months).
- **Day-Night Distinction:** During the day, expose your baby to natural light and engage in gentle play. At night, keep interactions quiet, lights dim, and focus on feeding and returning to sleep.
- **"Drowsy but Awake" (from 6-12 weeks):** You can gently try placing your baby in their cot when they are drowsy but still awake. If they grizzle for a minute or two, observe if they can settle. If they become distressed, pick them up and comfort them before trying again.

## When to Seek Help
- If your baby is unusually sleepy, hard to wake, or not feeding enough.
- If you have concerns about your baby's health, such as jaundice or signs of infection.
- If you're feeling overwhelmed, frustrated, or angry due to broken sleep. Reach out to your child and family health nurse or a parenting helpline for support.
