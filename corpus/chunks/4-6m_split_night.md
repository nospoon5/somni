# The Split Night: When Your Baby Is Awake and Happy at 2am

A "split night" is when a baby sleeps well for the first part of the night (e.g. 7pm–11pm or 7pm–1am), then wakes up fully awake, calm, and sometimes even playful or cooing — for one to three hours — before going back to sleep until morning. It's different from fussing or crying; the baby seems genuinely awake and alert, as if their body believes it's daytime.

## Why It Happens

A split night is almost always a **schedule problem, not a hunger or pain problem.** The most common cause is a mismatch between how much sleep the baby gets during the day and how much sleep pressure they have built by bedtime.

When babies nap too much (or too late) during the day, their circadian system doesn't have enough "sleep debt" built up to sustain a full night of consolidated sleep. The result is an involuntary mid-night awakening that can last 1–3 hours.

Other triggers include:

- **Bedtime too early relative to daytime sleep:** If a baby naps until 5pm and then goes to bed at 7pm, that's only 2 hours of wake time — often insufficient.
- **Too many naps or naps running too long for the age band**
- **A shift in sleep needs** as the baby matures (common around 4–6 months as sleep cycles reorganize)

## Key Points

- The defining feature of a split night: baby is genuinely alert and content, not distressed. If they're crying, look for a different cause (hunger, discomfort, temperature).
- Split nights are a reliable signal that nap timing or total daytime sleep needs adjustment — not that the baby needs more nighttime intervention.
- Responding by feeding, rocking, or entertaining during the split can make it longer, not shorter, because it provides stimulation that reinforces the waking window.

## What to Try

1. **Audit total daytime sleep:** For 4–6 month olds, target 3–4 hours total daytime sleep across 3 naps. If they're regularly hitting 4.5–5 hours in the day, a split night is almost predictable.
2. **Move the last nap earlier** or cap it sooner so the final nap ends no later than 4:30–5:00pm, giving more wake window before bedtime.
3. **Shift bedtime slightly later** (by 15–30 minutes) if the gap between last nap end and bedtime is too short. A 2–2.5 hour final wake window is typical for this age.
4. **Do not engage during the split:** If they are content in the cot, leave them. They will often go back to sleep without intervention. Going in and picking them up teaches the brain that this is an interactive time.
5. **Darken the room fully:** Sometimes a split night is lengthened by ambient light creeping in. Check the room is genuinely dark.

## What This Is Not

- A split night is not hunger (unless the baby is genuinely crying and feeding resolves it completely)
- It is not a sign of pain or illness (a sick baby will cry, not coo)
- It is not a sign of insufficient food — check if it resolves when you adjust nap timing before adding a feed

## When to Seek Help

- If adjusting the nap schedule does not resolve the split night within 5–7 days of consistent changes, it may be worth speaking with your child health nurse about the full sleep schedule.
- If the split night is accompanied by crying and distress rather than alertness, investigate pain (reflux, teething, illness) before adjusting the schedule.
