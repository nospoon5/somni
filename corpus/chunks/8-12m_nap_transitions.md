# Nap Transitions: Maintaining Two Naps (8-12 Months)

Between 8 and 12 months, most babies have settled into a pattern of two daytime naps, typically lasting up to two hours each. Total sleep for this age group usually ranges from 11 to 14 hours over a 24-hour period, with night-time sleep becoming more consolidated. While the transition from two naps to one is a significant milestone, it generally occurs later, often around 15 months of age.

During these months, your baby is experiencing rapid motor and cognitive development, such as crawling, pulling to stand, and increased awareness of their surroundings. These exciting milestones can sometimes impact sleep, leading to temporary changes in their routine or increased night waking. Maintaining a consistent and responsive approach to their sleep needs is key to supporting their development and ensuring restful periods for everyone.

## Key Points
- Babies aged 8-12 months typically have two daytime naps.
- Total sleep usually ranges from 11-14 hours in 24 hours.
- The transition from two naps to one usually happens after 12 months, often around 15 months.
- Developmental leaps can temporarily affect sleep patterns during this period.

## What to Try
- **Maintain a Predictable Routine:** A consistent feed, play, and sleep pattern, along with a calming bedtime routine, helps your baby anticipate sleep times.
- **Watch for Tired Cues:** Respond promptly to signs of tiredness to prevent your baby from becoming overtired, which can make settling more difficult.
- **Create a Conducive Sleep Environment:** Ensure the sleep space is quiet, dimly lit, and comfortable to support longer, more restorative naps.
- **Responsive Settling:** Continue to offer comfort and reassurance when your baby wakes or struggles to settle, adapting your support as they learn to self-settle.
- **Flexibility is Important:** Be prepared for slight adjustments to the routine during growth spurts, teething, or illness, and gently guide them back to their usual pattern afterwards.

## When to Seek Help
- If your baby consistently struggles with sleep, appears overtired, or if their sleep patterns are causing significant stress for your family.
- If you notice persistent changes in their sleep that don't seem related to developmental milestones or temporary disruptions.
- Consult your child and family health nurse or GP for personalised advice and to rule out any underlying health concerns.
