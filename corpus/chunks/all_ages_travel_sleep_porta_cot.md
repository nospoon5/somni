# Sleep Away from Home: Travel and Port-a-Cots

Almost every baby will have a rocky night or two when their sleep environment changes. This is completely normal and very temporary. The goal isn't perfect sleep — it's getting back to your routine as quickly as possible when you're home.

## Why It Happens

Babies are wired to notice environmental changes. A different smell, different darkness level, different sounds, and — most importantly — the absence of their usual sleep cues all combine to disrupt the sleep they have built at home. The good news is that the sleep skills they have learned are not lost. They are just disrupted by context.

## Key Points

- One to three rough nights when you arrive or return home is typical. By night three to four, most babies at least partially recover their usual pattern.
- Replicating their sleep *environment* matters more than replicating their sleep *location*. Bring the travel white noise machine, bring their sleep sack, bring their usual comforter (if age-appropriate).
- A good port-a-cot / travel cot is fine for sleep. According to Red Nose Australia, it must have: a firm, flat mattress, a safe sleep surface, and nothing loose inside — the same rules as their cot at home.
- **Do not accept second-hand or borrowed port-a-cots unless you are certain of the mattress quality.** An old or sagging mattress in a travel cot is a safe sleeping risk.

## What to Try

- **Bring their sleep environment:** Pack the white noise machine, their sleep sack, and if they have an approved comforter (12 months+), bring it.
- **Set up the port-a-cot in a dark space:** Hang a portable blackout blind or use a travel blackout solution on the window. Darkness is one of the highest-impact levers for travel sleep.
- **Protect their bedtime window:** When travelling across time zones, shift bedtime by no more than 30 minutes per day toward the local time.
- **Run the bedtime routine as normal:** Bath, book, feed — do it in exactly the same order you would at home. The routine is the cue, not the location.
- **Stay firm on the first night:** If they are settled in their safe sleep space, try not to bring them into your bed on night one. Giving in immediately teaches them that the travel cot is a stepping stone to your bed.
- **Give them time to habituate:** If they fuss on arrival, give them 10–15 minutes to settle before intervening. Often babies just need to orient to the new space.

## Returning Home

Expect one to two nights of adjustment when you return home too. This is normal — their brain needs to re-orient to their usual cues. Hold your routine firmly from the first night back.

## When to Seek Help

- If your baby had solid independent sleep skills before travel and they have not recovered after 5–7 nights back home, this may be unrelated to travel. Consider what else may have changed (illness, developmental leap, growth spurt).
