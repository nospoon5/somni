# Toddler Bedtime Stalling and Room-Leaving

"One more hug." "I need water." "I have to tell you something." These are the hallmarks of toddler bedtime stalling — and they are not signs of a bad sleeper. They are signs of a developmentally normal toddler who has discovered bedtime is flexible.

If your toddler is repeatedly leaving their room or calling out after you leave, consistency is the only tool that works. Not firmness — *consistency*.

## Why Toddlers Stall

Toddlers between 18 months and 4 years are in a developmental phase characterised by boundary-testing, preference for autonomy, and separation anxiety. Bedtime is a natural flashpoint because it requires them to:

1. Stop doing something enjoyable
2. Be separated from their person (you)
3. Go somewhere quiet and dark
4. Wait for sleep to come on its own

All of these things are developmentally hard for toddlers. Stalling is them trying to solve this problem with the tools they have (charm, requests, protests).

## The Fundamental Problem

Every time you return and grant a request — water, an extra story, another hug — you accidentally teach your toddler that **stalling works.** The next night, they stall a little more because the data says it's worth trying.

This is not a character flaw. It is operant conditioning working exactly as designed.

## What to Try

**Cap the bedtime routine firmly:**
- Decide on the routine length (e.g. bath, 2 books, hug, goodnight) and hold it. If they ask for a third book, name it: "We do two books. That's our deal."
- Give them *limited* control within the routine: "Which two books?" Not "How many books?"

**Announce the end of the routine clearly:**
- "This is the last hug. I'll see you in the morning. I love you."
- Then leave without a slow, drawn-out exit.

**The silent return (for room-leavers):**
- When your toddler leaves the room, return them without drama. No lecture, no eye contact, no negotiation. Pick them up or take their hand, walk them back, say "bed time," leave.
- This is boring enough to not be rewarding, but responsive enough to be safe.
- It takes 10–20 returns on night one for many toddlers. Night five is usually much better.

**The "check-in" promise:**
- "If you stay in bed, I'll come back and check on you in 2 minutes." Then do it. Then 4 minutes. Then 8 minutes. This builds trust and reduces the anxiety that fuels calling out.

**The ticket system (for verbal toddlers, 2.5+):**
- Give them 1–2 "tickets" per night they can use to call you for a legitimate reason (water, worry, hug). Once the tickets are gone, no more calls. This gives them agency and sets a clear limit.

**A door gate:**
- For toddlers in a big bed who repeatedly leave the room, a safety gate on the bedroom door is a practical option. It keeps them safely in their room without you needing to police it. It is not punitive — frame it as: "Your door gate helps you stay safe in your room while you fall asleep."

## Key Points

- Inconsistency is the enemy. One parent holding the line and the other caving undermines all progress.
- Every exception — even on hard nights — resets the learning.
- Expect it to get slightly worse before it gets better (extinction burst) in the first 1–2 nights.

## When to Seek Help

- If bedtime resistance is accompanied by severe distress (sustained screaming for 60+ minutes, vomiting from crying) consistently, speak to your child health nurse.
- If you suspect underlying anxiety is driving the bedtime resistance (not just toddler testing), your GP can refer you to a paediatric psychologist.
