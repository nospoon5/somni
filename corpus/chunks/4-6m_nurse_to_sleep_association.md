# Nurse To Sleep Association

Feeding to sleep is not a bad habit by default. At 4-6 months, it only becomes a problem when the baby now expects the breast to recreate sleep at **every** wake and the parent wants a different pattern.

The goal is not "never feed near sleep again". The goal is to separate **some** falling asleep from **all** feeding.

## How To Tell This Is The Issue

This chunk is a strong match when:

- bedtime only works on the breast
- naps and bedtime look different and the parent wants to know why
- the baby wakes soon after bedtime and seems to want the same feeding setup again
- the baby does not do a full hunger feed at every wake, but still needs the breast to drift off

## What To Change First

Start with bedtime, not every nap and every night wake all at once. Bedtime has the strongest sleep pressure and is usually the easiest place to create a new pattern.

## What To Do Tonight

- **Move the last feed slightly earlier in the routine.** Even 5-10 minutes earlier is a real start.
- **After the feed, keep one settling step that is not feeding:** cuddle, song, patting, or hand-on-chest in the cot.
- **If the baby protests, settle first before offering another feed immediately.** You are testing whether they need help to sleep or more milk.
- **If a wake happens very soon after bedtime, treat that first wake differently from a true overnight hunger feed.** Use your settling plan first if the feed was recent and substantial.

## Bedtime Vs Nap Difference

Many babies can feed to sleep for naps for a while and still manage bedtime better, or the reverse. Do not assume you must fix both together. If bedtime is the main pain point, work there first and leave naps more supported for now.

## What To Stop Doing

- Do not repeatedly unlatch, re-latch, and half-settle in a loop for an hour
- Do not remove the feed and every other comfort at the same time
- Do not call every short wake "hunger" if the baby just had a full bedtime feed
- Do not judge the plan after one rough bedtime

## A Balanced Stepping-Stone Plan

1. Feed
2. Burp or brief upright cuddle
3. One non-feeding settling step
4. Cot while drowsy, not fully out cold
5. Respond consistently if they protest

## When To Seek Help

- feeds are painful, very short, or clearly ineffective
- weight gain or milk transfer is a concern
- the baby seems uncomfortable lying flat, suggesting reflux or another medical issue rather than a pure sleep association
