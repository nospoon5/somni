# Night Terrors and Nightmares

Waking at 3am to your baby or toddler screaming as if terrified is one of the most alarming parenting experiences. Understanding whether it's a night terror, a nightmare, or something else entirely determines how you should respond — and they require very different approaches.

## Night Terrors vs Nightmares: The Key Difference

| | Night Terror | Nightmare |
|--|--|--|
| **Age** | More common 18 months–6 years (occasionally from ~10 months) | More common from 2 years+ as imagination develops |
| **State** | Eyes open, appears awake but is NOT conscious | Child is genuinely awake |
| **Memory** | No memory in the morning | May remember and talk about it |
| **Consolable?** | Very difficult — intervention can worsen it | Yes — child responds to comfort |
| **Duration** | 5–20 minutes | Shorter — settles with reassurance |
| **Timing** | Early-to-mid sleep cycle (first 1–2 hours) | Later in the night during REM sleep |

## What About Babies Under 12 Months?

True night terrors are uncommon in babies under 10–12 months. Screaming episodes in this age group are more often caused by:
- An incomplete arousal from deep sleep (partial waking)
- Overtiredness or a disrupted sleep schedule
- Pain (teeth, gas, reflux)
- A developmental leap causing more active and restless sleep

According to RCH Melbourne, night terrors are a partial arousal disorder and are most prevalent when a child is overtired or unwell.

## What to Do During a Night Terror

- **Do not try to wake them** — it can prolong or intensify the episode.
- **Do not pick them up or restrain them** — this can cause more distress.
- **Stay nearby** — your job is to keep them from injuring themselves (e.g. falling off a bed), not to comfort them consciously.
- **Keep your voice quiet and calm** — some gentle shushing or a soft "you're okay" may help, even if they can't consciously process it.
- **It will end on its own.** They will return to deep sleep. In the morning, they will have no memory of it.

## What to Do After a Nightmare

- Go to them, comfort them, and let them tell you about it if they want.
- Keep lights dim — you don't want full arousal.
- A brief comfort is usually enough. Prolonged engagement can wire the brain to expect a parent visit after every wake-up.

## Reducing Night Terror Frequency

- **Fix the sleep schedule:** Night terrors significantly increase when children are overtired. If you've had a late night, an early bedtime the following evening often prevents an episode.
- **Consistent bedtime:** Irregular bedtimes are one of the most common triggers.
- **Avoid waking them** in the hour before you go to bed (e.g., lifting them to take them to the toilet) — this disrupts the deep sleep stage that triggers terrors.

## When to Seek Help

- If night terrors occur nightly and are causing significant distress or are lasting more than 20 minutes, speak to your GP.
- If you observe any breath-holding that turns to blue lips, or your child appears in physical pain rather than upset — seek medical advice. These are not typical of night terrors.
- If nightmares are severe and recurrent and persist past the normal developmental window, a paediatric psychologist referral may help.
