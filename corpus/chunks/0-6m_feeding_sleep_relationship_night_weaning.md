# Feeding and Sleep in Babies (0-6 Months)

In the early months, your baby's sleep and feeding are closely linked, and frequent night waking for feeds is a completely normal and healthy part of their development. Newborns have tiny tummies that need regular refilling, meaning they'll wake often, day and night, for nourishment. This isn't a "bad habit" but a biological need.

Breastfeeding, in particular, can make night times a little easier. When your baby feeds, both of you release hormones like cholecystokinin (CCK) that promote sleepiness. Breastmilk also contains natural compounds like tryptophan and melatonin, which support your baby's developing sleep-wake rhythms. Many parents find that feeding their baby to sleep is a natural and effective way to provide comfort and help everyone drift back off quickly.

It's important to know that there's no evidence to suggest that introducing formula or solids early will help your baby sleep longer. In fact, some research indicates that breastfeeding parents may actually get more sleep overall than those who formula-feed. Your baby's sleep patterns will naturally evolve as they grow, with longer stretches of sleep typically emerging around 3-6 months as their circadian rhythm matures.

## Key Points
- Frequent night feeds are normal and essential for newborns due to their small stomach capacity.
- Feeding your baby to sleep is a natural and common way to provide comfort and nourishment.
- Breastfeeding releases hormones that promote sleepiness in both parent and baby.
- There is no evidence that early introduction of formula or solids improves infant sleep.
- Babies' sleep patterns are unique and will change as they mature; early sleep patterns don't last forever.

## What to Try
- **Respond to hunger cues:** Offer feeds when your baby shows signs of hunger, rather than strictly by the clock, especially in the early weeks.
- **Keep night feeds calm:** Use dim lighting and quiet tones during night feeds to help your baby understand the difference between day and night.
- **Keep baby close:** Having your baby sleep in the same room (in their own safe sleep space) can make night feeds quicker and easier, helping both of you get back to sleep faster.
- **Embrace feeding to sleep:** If it works for your family, don't worry about creating "bad habits." Your baby will learn other settling methods as they grow.

## When to Seek Help
- If your baby is very sleepy, difficult to wake for feeds, or not gaining weight adequately.
- If you are feeling overwhelmed, extremely exhausted, or experiencing symptoms of postnatal depression or anxiety due to broken sleep.
- If you have concerns about your baby's feeding patterns or overall health. Always consult your child health nurse or GP for personalised advice.
