# Contact Naps and Catnapping (7-12 Months)

As your baby grows from 7 to 12 months, their sleep patterns continue to evolve, often becoming more consolidated at night. However, daytime naps can still vary in length, and some babies may continue to have shorter naps (catnaps) or prefer contact naps. This age is also marked by significant developmental leaps, such as increased mobility and separation anxiety, which can sometimes impact sleep.

While your baby is becoming more aware and independent, their need for comfort and reassurance, especially around sleep, remains strong. Responsive settling techniques continue to be valuable, allowing you to support your baby's emotional needs while gently encouraging them towards more independent sleep skills. It's about finding a balance that works for your family, acknowledging that every baby is unique.

## Key Points
- **Developmental Impact:** New skills like crawling, pulling to stand, and separation anxiety can temporarily disrupt sleep patterns and make settling more challenging.
- **Naps Vary:** Daytime naps typically range from 30 minutes to 2 hours, and it's normal for some to be shorter.
- **Continued Need for Comfort:** Babies still need reassurance and closeness, especially when learning new sleep habits.
- **Gradual Independence:** This period is an ideal time to gently guide your baby towards self-settling, without resorting to methods that involve prolonged crying.

## What to Try
- **Consistent Bedtime Routine:** Establish a predictable and calming bedtime routine (e.g., bath, story, quiet cuddle, feed) to signal that it's time for sleep. Consistency helps your baby feel secure.
- **Responsive Settling:** Continue to respond to your baby's cues with comfort. This might involve gentle patting, rocking, or soothing words. As they become more settled, you can gradually reduce the amount of physical touch or your presence in the room.
- **Parental Presence:** If your baby struggles with separation, you can try staying in the room while they fall asleep. Sit or lie beside the cot, offering quiet reassurance, and gradually move further away over several nights or weeks.
- **Drowsy but Awake:** Place your baby in their cot when they are calm and drowsy, but not fully asleep. This gives them the opportunity to practice falling asleep independently.
- **Address Separation Anxiety:** Offer extra cuddles and reassurance during the day and before sleep. Let them know you're nearby if they call out, but encourage them to resettle themselves if they are just grizzling.
- **Phasing Out Night Feeds:** If your baby is growing and developing well, you can consider phasing out night feeds from 6 months for formula-fed babies, and from 12 months for breastfed babies, if you wish.

## When to Seek Help
- If your baby's sleep problems are causing significant stress or affecting your mental health.
- If your baby's sleep patterns suddenly change dramatically and don't improve after a week or two, especially if accompanied by other concerns like reduced feeding or alertness.
- Your child and family health nurse or GP can provide tailored advice and support.
