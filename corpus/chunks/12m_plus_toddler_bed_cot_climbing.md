# Cot Climbing and the Move to a Toddler Bed

If your toddler has climbed out of their cot for the first time, you don't need to immediately move to a toddler bed — but you do need to take it seriously as a safety issue.

## Should You Move to a Toddler Bed Now?

The short answer: **not necessarily, and often not immediately.** A toddler who has just started climbing has not necessarily outgrown the cot — they have just discovered they can get out. The relevant question is whether it's still *safe*.

According to Raising Children Network, the key indicators for moving to a toddler bed are:
- Your toddler can climb out even with the mattress at its lowest setting
- They have already landed on the floor (i.e. the risk is real, not theoretical)
- They are developmentally ready for the freedom of an open bed (usually 2.5–3 years is the sweet spot)

Moving too early (before 24 months) often trades one problem (cot escapes) for a more complex one (roaming the house at 3am).

## Before You Move — Try This First

- **Lower the mattress to its lowest position** if you haven't already.
- **Remove any objects they may be using as a step** (bumpers, large soft toys, a nearby dresser).
- **Use a sleeping bag** — it makes climbing significantly harder because their legs can't separate like ladder rungs.
- **React with zero excitement:** If they climb out and you come running and laughing, you have just trained a new game. Return them calmly, say "bed time," and leave.

## When You Do Transition

- Do it as a deliberate, planned decision — not in crisis mode after a fall at 2am.
- **Childproof the room fully** before the first night: stair gate on the door, dresser anchored to the wall, nothing climbable near windows.
- A toddler bed (with rails) is typically easier than going straight to a single bed for most 18–24 month olds.
- The same sleep rules apply: consistent bedtime routine, put down awake, respond calmly and boringly to any escapes.

## Managing Room-Leaving After Transition

Expect repeated exits in the first week. Use the "silent return" method: walk them back every time, no conversation, no eye contact, no lecture. It is tedious, but it is the fastest path to them understanding the boundary.

A door gate (stair gate on the bedroom door) is a practical and safe option for toddlers who will not stay put. It allows them to be awake in their room safely without accessing the rest of the house.

## When to Seek Help

- If your child falls from the cot: assess for any signs of head injury (unusual drowsiness, vomiting, inconsolable crying, unequal pupils). If in doubt, seek medical advice immediately.
- If room-leaving persists for more than two to three weeks with consistent management, speak to your child health nurse about behavioural strategies.
