# Feeding and Sleep in Newborns (0-3 Months)

In these early months, your baby's sleep and feeding are deeply intertwined, and frequent waking for feeds is completely normal. Newborns have tiny tummies that need regular refilling, both day and night, to support their rapid growth and development. Their internal body clocks are still maturing, so they don't yet distinguish between day and night for sleep.

It's very common for babies in this age group to fall asleep while feeding, whether at the breast or with a bottle. This isn't a "bad habit"; it's a natural and comforting way for them to drift off. Breastmilk also contains natural hormones like tryptophan and melatonin, which can help both you and your baby feel sleepy and settle back to rest more quickly after a night feed.

Responsive feeding, where you respond to your baby's hunger cues, is key during this period. Night feeds are essential for their nourishment and also play a vital role in establishing your milk supply if you are breastfeeding.

## Key Points
- Frequent night feeds are normal and necessary for newborns due to their small stomach capacity and rapid growth.
- Babies often fall asleep while feeding, which is a natural and comforting way for them to settle.
- Breastmilk contains hormones that can help promote sleep for both mother and baby.
- Newborns are still developing their day-night sleep patterns and will wake often.
- Night weaning is not recommended for babies in this age band.

## What to Try
- **Respond to hunger cues:** Offer feeds whenever your baby shows signs of hunger, rather than sticking to a strict schedule.
- **Keep night feeds calm:** Use dim lights and quiet tones during night feeds to help your baby understand that night-time is for sleep.
- **Feed to sleep:** If your baby drifts off while feeding, it's perfectly fine to let them.
- **Consider a 'top-up' feed:** If your baby wakes soon after a feed, a quick top-up might help them fall into a deeper sleep.
- **Skin-to-skin contact:** Holding your baby skin-to-skin can help them feel secure and promote feeding and sleep.

## When to Seek Help
- If your baby is unusually sleepy, very hard to wake, or not feeding enough (less than 8 times in 24 hours).
- If you have concerns about your baby's weight gain or overall health.
- If your breasts feel uncomfortably full and you need guidance on managing milk supply.
- If you are feeling overwhelmed or struggling to cope with broken sleep; support is available from your child and family health nurse or a parenting helpline.
