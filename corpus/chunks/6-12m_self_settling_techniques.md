# Self-Settling for Babies (6-12 Months)

As your baby grows from 6 to 12 months, their sleep patterns often become more consolidated at night, though waking is still a normal part of their development. During this period, babies are rapidly acquiring new motor skills like crawling and pulling to stand, and cognitive leaps such as object permanence and separation anxiety can temporarily impact their sleep. This is often when families consider more structured approaches to encourage independent settling.

While some methods involve periods of crying, many Australian health organisations, like Tresillian and the Australian Breastfeeding Association, advocate for responsive, gentle approaches that prioritise comfort and connection. Other sources discuss methods like "controlled comforting" or "camping out" as effective from around six months. The key is to find a consistent approach that aligns with your family's values and your baby's temperament, supporting them as they learn this important skill.

## Key Points
- Babies aged 6-12 months typically sleep 11-14 hours in 24 hours, with longer night sleeps and 1-2 daytime naps.
- Developmental milestones and separation anxiety can influence sleep patterns and settling.
- The goal is to gradually reduce parental intervention, empowering your baby to self-settle.
- Consistency in your chosen settling method is vital for success.

## What to Try
- **Consistent Bedtime Routine:** Maintain a predictable and calming bedtime routine. This might include a bath, gentle massage, pyjamas, reading stories, and quiet cuddles. This sequence helps signal to your baby that it's time to wind down for sleep.
- **"Drowsy but Awake":** Continue to place your baby in their cot when they are drowsy but still awake. This reinforces the association between their sleep space and falling asleep independently.
- **Gradually Reduce Settling Help:** Move from more hands-on settling to less direct support. You might start by patting or rocking until drowsy, then transition to verbal reassurance ("I'm here, time to sleep now") from beside the cot.
- **Parental Presence ("Camping Out"):** If your baby struggles with you leaving the room, consider "camping out." This involves sitting or lying next to their cot, offering comfort with your voice or gentle touch, and gradually moving your chair or mattress further from the cot over 1-3 weeks until you are out of the room.
- **Respond to Waking:** If your baby wakes and grizzles, pause to see if they can resettle themselves. If they start crying, respond with comfort, then return to your chosen settling method.
- **Dummy Independence:** If your baby uses a dummy, teach them to reinsert it themselves. Place their hand on the dummy and guide it to their mouth, saying "Get your dummy."
- **Phasing Out Sleep Habits:** If certain sleep associations (like feeding to sleep or constant rocking) are causing frequent night wakings, you can gradually phase them out. For formula-fed babies, night feeds can be considered for phasing out from 6 months; for breastfed babies, from 12 months, if you choose.
- **Bedtime Fading:** If your baby takes a long time to fall asleep, consider "bedtime fading." This involves starting your bedtime routine at the time your baby naturally falls asleep, then gradually moving it 15 minutes earlier every few days until you reach your desired bedtime.
- **Optimise Sleep Environment:** Ensure your baby's room is dark, quiet, and at a comfortable temperature to support uninterrupted sleep.

## When to Seek Help
- If your baby's sleep problems persist for 1-2 weeks despite consistent efforts, or if they are causing significant distress for your family.
- If you are experiencing severe exhaustion, anxiety, or symptoms of postnatal depression.
- If you suspect an underlying medical condition (e.g., ear infection, reflux, sleep apnoea) or developmental disorder is affecting your baby's sleep.
- If your baby is having frequent nightmares or night terrors. Consult your GP or child and family health nurse for guidance and potential referrals to a paediatrician or sleep specialist.
