# Self-Settling for Babies (3-6 Months)

As your baby moves into the 3-6 month age range, you might notice their sleep patterns becoming a little more predictable. Many babies start having longer stretches of sleep at night, sometimes up to 6 hours by six months, and daytime naps may consolidate into 2-3 longer periods. This is a wonderful time for gently encouraging self-settling skills, as your baby's circadian rhythm is maturing, and they're becoming more aware of their surroundings.

The goal remains responsive settling – meeting your baby's need for comfort while helping them learn to fall asleep independently. While some sleep training methods are introduced around 4-6 months, many Australian health organisations advocate for gentler approaches that minimise distress. It's about finding what feels right for your family and your baby's unique temperament.

## Key Points
- Babies in this age group are developing more regular sleep patterns and longer night sleeps.
- They are beginning to develop the ability to self-soothe and resettle themselves.
- Responsive settling techniques are encouraged to support this development gently.
- "Cry it out" methods are often advised against for babies under 4-6 months, with a preference for more supportive approaches.

## What to Try
- **Emphasise Day-Night Differences:** Continue to expose your baby to natural light and active play during the day. At night, keep the environment dim and quiet, focusing on feeds and returning to sleep without stimulating play.
- **"Drowsy but Awake":** Consistently place your baby in their cot when they are drowsy but still awake. This helps them learn to associate their sleep space with falling asleep, a key step towards self-soothing.
- **Observe and Respond:** If your baby grizzles or fusses when put down, wait a minute or two to see if they can settle themselves. If the grizzling turns into crying, offer comfort with gentle words, patting, or a brief cuddle until they calm, then return them to their cot.
- **Consistent Bedtime Routine:** Establish a calming bedtime routine that signals it's time for sleep. This might include a warm bath, a gentle massage, putting on pyjamas, reading a story, and quiet cuddles. Consistency helps your baby anticipate sleep.
- **Transition from Wrapping:** If you've been wrapping your baby, it's time to stop when they show the first signs of rolling (usually around 3-4 months). Transition to a safe infant sleeping bag with fitted neck and armholes and no hood.
- **Consider a "Dream Feed":** Some parents find a late-night "dream feed" (a top-up feed between 10 pm and midnight while your baby is still mostly asleep) can help them sleep for a longer stretch towards morning.

## When to Seek Help
- If your baby is consistently struggling to settle or sleep, and your chosen methods aren't showing improvement after a week or two.
- If you're feeling exhausted, overwhelmed, or if lack of sleep is impacting your mental health. Support is available from your child and family health nurse or parenting helplines.
- If you have any concerns about your baby's health, growth, or development that might be affecting their sleep.
