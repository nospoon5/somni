# Fear of the Dark and Using Nightlights

Fear of the dark typically emerges around 2–3 years of age. It's a normal developmental milestone tied to a toddler's growing imagination and new awareness of what they can't see or control. The goal is to validate the fear while not reinforcing it in ways that make sleep harder.

## Why It Starts Around This Age

Toddlers at 2–3 years develop what psychologists call "symbolic thinking" — they can now imagine what might be in the dark, even if they haven't seen it. This is healthy brain development, even if it's inconvenient at bedtime. The fear is real to them, even if the threat isn't real.

## The Nightlight Dilemma

The common trap: parent gives a nightlight, child asks for the door open, then asks for the hall light on, then for mum to stay. Each concession temporarily soothes the anxiety but teaches the brain: *"The dark IS dangerous — and I need more help to stay safe."* This escalates rather than resolves the fear.

The solution is to use a nightlight *strategically,* not as an unlimited accommodation.

## What to Try

- **Use a dim, warm-toned light:** Amber or red-spectrum nightlights are ideal. They provide enough light for orientation without suppressing melatonin (which blue/white light does). Place it low — at plug-socket height — and out of direct sightline from the pillow.
- **Keep the door closed (or mostly closed):** Hall light seeping under the door is often more stimulating than a well-placed nightlight. A consistent, dim room is better than variable light from another room.
- **Name the fear and normalise it:** "I know the dark feels a bit scary. Lots of kids feel that way. That's why we have our nightlight." Don't minimise ("there's nothing to be scared of") or overdramatise ("you're right, it IS a bit spooky").
- **Introduce a comfort object:** A soft toy or comforter that is "brave" or "looks after" the toddler at night can be genuinely helpful. The object gives them something to direct the anxiety toward.
- **Check-in reassurance (briefly):** Tell them you will check on them in a few minutes. Then actually check. This builds trust. Over 5–7 nights, extend the check-in window.
- **Avoid monster-proofing rituals:** Spraying "monster spray" or checking wardrobes can backfire — it confirms to the toddler that checking is necessary, which validates the threat.

## Holding the Boundary

If your toddler is using fear of the dark to stall bedtime or invite you to stay, gently acknowledge the feeling and hold the limit: *"I know you don't love the dark. That's what your nightlight is for. I'll check on you in two minutes."* Then follow through and leave.

Spending an hour in the room does not resolve the fear — it teaches that expressing the fear earns your presence.

## When to Seek Help

- If the fear is so severe it causes significant distress across all parts of the day (not just bedtime), mention it to your GP.
- If fear of the dark continues to significantly disrupt sleep past age 4–5, a paediatric psychologist referral may be worth exploring.
