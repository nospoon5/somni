# Nap Transitions: From Three to Two Naps (4-7 Months)

Around 4 to 7 months of age, many babies begin to transition from taking three daytime naps to two. This shift is a natural part of their developing sleep patterns and often coincides with longer wake windows and more consolidated night sleep. While every baby is unique, this period typically sees total sleep ranging from 12 to 15 hours over a 24-hour cycle.

You might notice your little one showing signs of readiness for this transition, such as resisting their third nap, taking shorter naps, or having difficulty falling asleep for a nap they previously enjoyed. Their awake periods, or "wake windows," may also extend from around 1.5-2 hours to 2-2.5 hours. It's a time of significant development, and adjusting their sleep schedule can help ensure they continue to get the rest they need without becoming overtired.

## Key Points
- Babies aged 4-7 months typically sleep 12-15 hours in a 24-hour period.
- The transition from three to two naps usually occurs within this age range.
- Signs of readiness include resisting a nap, shorter naps, or longer wake windows.
- Consistency in routines is crucial during this developmental phase.

## What to Try
- **Observe Your Baby's Cues:** Pay close attention to tired signs like yawning, eye-rubbing, or fussiness. These cues are your best guide for when to offer a nap.
- **Gradually Adjust the Schedule:** If your baby is resisting their third nap, try shifting their morning and afternoon naps slightly later to accommodate longer wake windows.
- **Prioritise Two Solid Naps:** Focus on making the first two naps of the day as restorative as possible. A consistent pre-nap routine can help signal it's time to rest.
- **Maintain a Consistent Bedtime:** A predictable bedtime routine helps regulate your baby's internal clock and can make the transition smoother.
- **Responsive Settling:** Continue to respond to your baby's needs for comfort and reassurance as they adjust to new sleep patterns.

## When to Seek Help
- If your baby seems consistently overtired or distressed despite your efforts to adjust their nap schedule.
- If you have concerns about your baby's overall sleep, feeding, or development.
- Your child and family health nurse or GP can offer tailored advice and support.
