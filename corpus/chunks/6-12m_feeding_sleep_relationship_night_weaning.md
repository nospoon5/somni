# Feeding and Sleep in Older Babies (6-12 Months)

As your baby approaches 6 months and beyond, their sleep and feeding needs continue to evolve. While night waking is still common and normal for comfort and connection, the need for frequent night feeds for hunger may start to decrease, especially for formula-fed babies.

For formula-fed babies from around 6 months, if they are growing and developing well, you might consider gradually phasing out night feeds. Formula is digested more slowly than breastmilk, so hunger is less likely to be the primary reason for waking at this age.

For breastfed babies, night weaning is generally considered from 12 months of age, as earlier weaning can impact your milk supply. However, if you're comfortable with night feeding, there's no rush to stop. Breastfeeding continues to offer comfort and can help both you and your baby settle back to sleep.

Introducing solids around 6 months is an important developmental step, but it's unlikely to make your baby sleep longer. Solids should complement, not replace, milk feeds. As your baby gets older, you might notice they become more distractible during daytime feeds, sometimes "postponing" feeds until the night. Offering extra daytime feeds can help balance their intake.

A "dream feed" – a late-night top-up feed before you go to bed – is an option some parents find helpful to encourage longer sleep stretches. This involves gently rousing your baby for a feed without fully waking them.

## Key Points
- Night waking is normal for babies over 6 months, often for comfort rather than hunger.
- Night weaning for formula-fed babies can be considered from 6 months if they are growing well.
- Night weaning for breastfed babies is typically considered from 12 months to protect milk supply.
- Introducing solids does not necessarily lead to longer sleep.
- Feeding to sleep is a common and acceptable settling method; it won't create "bad habits."

## What to Try
- **Assess readiness:** Before considering night weaning, consult your child and family health nurse or GP to ensure it's appropriate for your baby's individual growth and development.
- **Gradual reduction (Formula-fed, 6+ months):** If your baby drinks more than 60ml per night feed, gradually reduce the volume by 20-30ml every second night over 5-7 nights until the feed is eliminated.
- **Gradual reduction (Breastfed, 12+ months):** If your baby feeds for more than 5 minutes at night, gradually reduce feeding time by 2-5 minutes every second night over 5-7 nights. For shorter feeds, you might stop altogether.
- **Responsive settling:** After reducing or eliminating a feed, use gentle settling techniques like patting, rocking, or verbal reassurance to help your baby resettle without feeding.
- **Positive bedtime routine:** Establish a consistent, calming bedtime routine that doesn't solely rely on feeding to sleep.
- **Dream feed:** Consider a late-night feed (10 pm-midnight) to see if it helps your baby sleep longer.

## When to Seek Help
- If your baby becomes very distressed or upset during night weaning attempts.
- If you are struggling with the process or feeling overwhelmed.
- If you have concerns about your baby's weight gain, development, or overall health.
- If you are experiencing symptoms of postnatal depression or anxiety. Your child and family health nurse or GP can provide support and guidance.
