# Bridging the 2 To 1 Nap Transition Before 12 Months

The biggest mistake in this transition is going cold turkey too early. Many babies flirt with one nap well before they can actually cope with it every day. An 11-month-old refusing a nap is not the same as a toddler who is genuinely ready to live on one nap most days.

## Most Babies Are Not Ready To Go Cold Turkey At 11 Months

The 2 to 1 nap transition usually lands closer to the second year than parents expect. Before that point, babies often look "not tired enough" for one nap and then become wildly overtired by late afternoon if you force it.

## Readiness Cues

### Probably not ready yet

- the morning nap still lands easily most days
- bedtime collapses after a one-nap day
- the midday nap is not long enough to carry the whole day
- early morning waking or split nights appear when you push the first nap too late

### Closer to ready

- one of the two naps is being refused most days for 1-2 weeks
- the second nap keeps drifting so late it wrecks bedtime
- the child can comfortably stay awake much longer in the morning without melting down
- the midday nap is becoming the longest and most reliable nap

Some babies nearing 12 months still need **bridging**, not a full one-nap schedule every single day. Once a child is clearly in the 12 months+ range, use the toddler nap-transition chunk as the primary retrieval target.

## Bridging Options

### Option 1: The short morning bridge

Use a 10-15 minute car, pram, or contact doze in the morning, then protect a proper midday nap. This is often the cleanest fix when they are too tired to reach lunch but not ready for a full morning nap.

### Option 2: Cap nap one, keep nap two

If the morning nap still helps but is pushing the afternoon too late, cap the first nap hard and keep the second one as the restoring nap.

### Option 3: Alternate days

Use 2 naps on rougher days and 1 nap on stronger days. This is very common during the messy middle and is often more realistic than forcing a perfect textbook schedule.

## What To Do Tonight

- If today was a failed one-nap day, do **not** read that as proof they should stay awake even longer tomorrow.
- Use an earlier bedtime tonight if the second half of the day was stretched.
- Decide tomorrow's plan before morning. Randomly changing between "maybe one nap" and "maybe two naps" by feel leads to overtiredness.

## A Practical Starting Point

- If they wake tired and crashy by mid-morning: use a tiny bridge nap, not a full first nap
- If the first nap is still solid and the second is the only problem: cap the first nap
- If they are truly refusing one nap most days: move the main nap later gradually, not in one huge jump

## What To Stop Doing

- Do not force one nap every day just because they are 12 or 13 months old
- Do not let a long first nap "accidentally" become the whole plan if bedtime then implodes
- Do not judge readiness from one daycare day, one car nap, or one bad nap day

## When To Seek Help

- the child is sleeping very little across 24 hours for age and staying highly unsettled
- the midday nap never consolidates and nights are also poor
- schedule changes keep producing extreme overtiredness rather than gradual adaptation
