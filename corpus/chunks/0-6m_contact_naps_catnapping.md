# Contact Naps and Catnapping (0-6 Months)

For babies aged 0 to 6 months, contact naps and shorter sleep periods, often called catnapping, are a completely normal part of their development. Your little one needs frequent feeds and comfort, and being close to you helps them feel safe and secure.

Newborns, in particular, have very short sleep cycles, typically lasting between 20 to 60 minutes. They spend a lot of time in "active sleep," where they move and make noises, making them easier to rouse. It's common for them to wake at the end of a sleep cycle and need a bit of help to transition into the next one. This means their sleep is often spread across both day and night, rather than in long stretches.

As your baby approaches 3-4 months, they start to develop a clearer day-night rhythm, and their sleep cycles may lengthen slightly. However, catnapping can still be common, and responsive settling techniques remain key to supporting their sleep. Feeding to sleep is also a natural and effective way for many babies to drift off, and it doesn't create "bad habits."

## Key Points
- **Normal Development:** Short sleep cycles (20-60 minutes) are typical for young babies, leading to frequent waking and shorter naps.
- **Closeness is Key:** Babies thrive on physical contact and reassurance, especially during sleep.
- **No "Bad Habits":** Feeding or holding your baby to sleep is a natural comfort for them and won't spoil them.
- **Day-Night Rhythm:** Around 3-4 months, babies begin to distinguish between day and night, which can help consolidate night sleep.

## What to Try
- **Responsive Settling:** When your baby stirs or wakes, respond gently. This might involve holding them in your arms, gentle rocking, rhythmic patting, or soft "shhh" sounds.
- **Drowsy but Awake:** From around 3 months, try placing your baby down when they are drowsy but still awake. This helps them learn to associate their cot with falling asleep.
- **Emphasise Day and Night:** During the day, keep things bright and active. At night, keep the environment dim, quiet, and interactions minimal to help them learn the difference.
- **Wrapping:** If your baby is not yet showing signs of rolling, a lightweight cotton or muslin wrap can help them feel secure and reduce startling reflexes that might wake them. Discontinue wrapping once they show signs of rolling (usually 3-4 months).
- **Safe Co-sleeping:** If you find yourself falling asleep while feeding or comforting, ensure your sleep space is set up safely to minimise risks.

## When to Seek Help
- If your baby is excessively sleepy, hard to wake, not feeding enough, or not gaining weight as expected.
- If you are struggling with extreme exhaustion, anxiety, or frustration due to your baby's sleep patterns. Your GP or child and family health nurse can offer support and guidance.
