# Nap Transitions: 0-3 Months

In these early months, your newborn's sleep is a beautiful, ever-changing landscape, not yet defined by strict nap schedules or transitions. Babies are still learning the difference between day and night, and their sleep patterns are quite unique.

Newborns typically sleep a lot, often between 14 to 17 hours over a 24-hour period, but this sleep is usually broken into many short bursts. You might notice your little one having 6 to 8 periods of sleep, each lasting anywhere from 20 minutes to 4 hours, spread across both day and night. This frequent waking is normal, as their tiny tummies need regular feeds, and their brains are rapidly developing during active sleep.

Around 4 weeks of age, you might start to see a subtle shift as their day-night rhythm begins to emerge. They may have slightly longer awake times and start to consolidate some sleep at night, but frequent waking for feeds and comfort is still very much expected. There isn't a formal "nap transition" at this stage; instead, it's about observing your baby's cues and responding with warmth and flexibility.

## Key Points
- Newborns sleep 14-17 hours in 24 hours, in short, irregular bursts.
- Sleep is spread across day and night, with no clear pattern initially.
- Frequent waking for feeds and comfort is normal and healthy.
- Day-night rhythms begin to emerge around 4 weeks.

## What to Try
- **Follow a flexible "feed, play, sleep" rhythm:** Offer a feed when they wake, engage in gentle play, then settle them back to sleep when tired.
- **Respond to tired signs promptly:** Look for yawning, frowning, jerky movements, or fussiness. Settling them before they become overtired can make it easier.
- **Emphasise day and night differences:** Keep daytime bright and active, and night-time dim and quiet, even during feeds.
- **Gentle settling techniques:** Hold, rock, pat, or sing softly to help your baby feel secure and drift off to sleep. You can't spoil a newborn with comfort.
- **Prioritise night feeds for sleep:** At night, focus on feeding and quickly settling back to sleep, keeping interactions minimal and calm.

## When to Seek Help
- If your baby is unusually sleepy or hard to wake, especially if they are not feeding enough (at least 8 times in 24 hours).
- If you are concerned about your baby's weight gain or overall health.
- If you are feeling overwhelmed, exhausted, or struggling to cope with broken sleep. Reach out to your child health nurse or GP for support.
