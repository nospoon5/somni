# Recognising Newborn Tired Signs

It's common for newborns to quickly transition from being happily awake to suddenly overtired and inconsolable. Spotting your baby's sleepy cues early is the secret to easier settling. 

Babies communicate their tiredness long before they start crying. Catching these early, subtle signs makes a huge difference, because an overtired baby releases stress hormones like cortisol, which actually fight off sleep.

## Key Points
- **Early signs (spotting these is your goal):** Include avoiding eye contact, yawning, jerky arm or leg movements, clenching fists, or sudden jerky motions.
- **Late signs (overtiredness):** Full-blown crying, arching the back backwards, stiffness, and difficulty calming down. Crying is often the very last sign a baby is tired.
- **Newborn sleep limits:** In the first three months, newborn wake windows are extremely short. A newborn may only stay happily awake for 45 to 90 minutes between sleeps.

## What to Try
- **Watch the clock and your baby**: Instead of relying purely on signs, keep an eye on how long they've been awake. If it's been an hour, start looking closely for those early cues.
- **Start winding down immediately**: As soon as you see a tired sign (avoiding gaze, pulling at ears, yawning), move your baby to their quiet, dim sleep space. You want to settle them *before* they start crying.
- **Swaddle and settle**: A tired newborn often finds their own jerky movements startling. A secure (but hip-safe) swaddle can help calm those reflexes and prepare them for sleep.

## When to Seek Help
- While resisting sleep and getting overtired is very normal for newborns, if your baby is crying inconsolably for hours, seems in pain when lying flat, or is entirely unable to be settled by feeding, rocking, or holding, speak to your GP or child health nurse.
