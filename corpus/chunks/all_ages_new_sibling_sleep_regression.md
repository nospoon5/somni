# Sleep Regression After a New Sibling

A toddler who was sleeping well suddenly needing you at bedtime again after a new baby arrives is one of the most common parenting challenges — and one of the most understandable. They are not being manipulative. They are processing a genuinely seismic change to their world.

## Why This Happens

Toddlers don't have the vocabulary or emotional regulation to say "I feel displaced and I'm anxious about getting less of you." Instead, they show it through sleep. Wanting you in the room at bedtime, calling out, waking at night — these are attachment behaviours, and they almost always spike when family structure changes.

This is normal. It is time-limited. And it does not undo their sleep training.

## Key Points

- A toddler regressing after a new sibling is not the same as a toddler who never learned to sleep. The skills are there — they're just temporarily buried under stress.
- The regression is usually stronger in the first 3–6 weeks and gradually improves as the new family rhythm becomes familiar.
- **You cannot pour from an empty cup.** Managing both babies' sleep simultaneously is genuinely hard. Prioritise what's safest and most sustainable for your family.

## What to Try

- **Protect the toddler's one-on-one time:** Even 15 minutes of undivided, toddler-chosen activity each day without the baby significantly helps. This doesn't have to happen at bedtime — it could be bath time, breakfast, or an afternoon activity.
- **Keep their bedtime routine as consistent as possible:** The routine itself is reassuring. Even if it slips by 15–20 minutes some nights, keep the *sequence* the same: bath, book, cuddle, bed.
- **Involve them in being a big kid:** "Big kids sleep in their own beds" can be a gentle positive frame — not a pressure tactic, but an acknowledgement of their new identity.
- **If you've been sitting in the room:** Decide whether this is sustainable. If you can hold it for 3–6 weeks without it becoming entrenched, it may be the path of least resistance during the acute phase. If it's already been 3+ weeks and it's not improving, begin a gradual withdrawal.
- **Gradual withdrawal approach:** Sit in the room for 3–4 nights, then move the chair to the doorway for 3–4 nights, then just outside the door. This gives them visible reassurance without requiring your physical presence.
- **Tag team if possible:** If one parent can handle the toddler bedtime while the other handles the baby, this reduces the toddler's sense of "competing" with the baby for you.

## For the Baby

Newborns who arrive into a family with a toddler often have slightly more disrupted sleep early on due to the household being noisier and less predictable. This is normal and resolves as the schedule settles.

## When to Seek Help

- If the regression is still significant after 6–8 weeks post-arrival with consistent management, it may be worth a session with a child health nurse.
- If the toddler shows significant daytime anxiety (clinginess, regression in other areas like toilet training) alongside the sleep regression, speak to your GP about whether additional support would help.
