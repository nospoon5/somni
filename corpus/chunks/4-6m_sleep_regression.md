# Understanding the 4-Month Sleep Regression

Around four months of age, many babies experience a shift in their sleep patterns, often referred to as the "4-month sleep regression." This is a normal developmental phase where your baby's sleep cycles begin to mature, becoming more like an adult's. While this is a positive sign of development, it can temporarily lead to more frequent night waking and shorter naps.

At this age, babies typically sleep 14-15 hours over a 24-hour period, with sleep cycles lasting around 30-50 minutes. They may wake more easily during lighter sleep stages and need help to transition into the next sleep cycle. You might notice your baby becoming more aware of their surroundings and starting to develop a clearer day-night rhythm. This increased awareness, combined with changing sleep architecture, can make settling more challenging.

It's common for babies to still wake for feeds at night during this period. Remember, this phase is temporary and not a reflection of anything you're doing wrong. Consistency and responsive support are key to navigating these changes.

## Key Points
- The 4-month sleep regression is a normal developmental stage linked to maturing sleep cycles.
- Babies' sleep cycles become shorter (30-50 minutes) and more adult-like, leading to more frequent waking.
- Day-night sleep patterns begin to emerge, but night waking for feeds is still typical.
- Increased awareness of their environment can make it harder for babies to settle.

## What to Try
- **Be Consistent and Responsive:** Continue to respond to your baby's tired signs promptly to prevent them from becoming overtired, which can make settling harder.
- **Emphasise Day-Night Differences:** Keep night-time interactions quiet, dimly lit, and focused on feeding and settling. Reserve active play and bright lights for daytime.
- **Gentle Settling Techniques:** Use soothing in-arms or hands-on settling methods like gentle rhythmic patting, rocking, or soft singing to help your baby calm and fall asleep.
- **Drowsy but Awake:** When possible, try placing your baby in their cot drowsy but awake. This can help them learn to associate their cot with falling asleep and develop self-soothing skills.
- **Increase Support if Needed:** It's okay to increase your level of support during this time, knowing that "this too shall pass."

## When to Seek Help
- If your baby's sleep changes are accompanied by signs of illness, such as fever, reduced feeding, or unusual lethargy, consult your doctor or child health nurse.
- If you are feeling overwhelmed, exhausted, or experiencing symptoms of postnatal depression or anxiety due to sleep challenges, reach out to your GP, child and family health nurse, or a parenting helpline for support.
