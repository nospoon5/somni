# Separation Anxiety And Sleep

If a child sleeps better with you present but falls apart the moment you leave, think separation anxiety before you assume the whole schedule is wrong. This is especially common around 8-10 months, after illness or travel, when daycare changes, or after a new sibling arrives.

The key mistake is swinging between two extremes:

- disappearing too fast for a child who is genuinely anxious
- staying so long every night that the new pattern becomes the only way they can fall asleep

## How To Tell It Is Separation Anxiety

This chunk is a strong match when:

- bedtime protests spike when the parent stands up or heads to the door
- naps can sometimes work with another caregiver, but bedtime with the main attachment figure is harder
- nights worsened after a major change rather than after a clear schedule change
- the child wants proximity more than feeding or play

## What To Do Tonight

- **Make the goodbye clear, calm, and brief.** Sneaking out often increases checking and panic.
- **Use a predictable return pattern.** A simple check-in promise or camping-out setup usually works better than random popping in and out.
- **Keep the routine steady.** Separation anxiety improves with predictability, not with a longer and longer bedtime.

## Best Fit Responses

### Mild separation wobble

Use brief check-ins with the same phrase each time. Keep the room dark and the interaction short.

### Strong separation anxiety

Camping out is usually the cleaner option. Stay visible, reduce your help gradually, and move farther away every few nights.

### Triggered by a change like a new sibling or daycare

Add connection in the daytime, not by accidentally building a 90-minute bedtime. Ten focused minutes of one-on-one time earlier in the day often helps more than endless bedtime negotiation.

## What To Stop Doing

- do not sneak out once they are asleep every single night if you want independent settling
- do not introduce three new rules at once
- do not confuse reassurance with endless talking, lights, and extra stories
- do not assume every anxious bedtime means the child needs less sleep

## When To Seek Help

- the child is panicking so hard that the current plan is clearly too abrupt
- anxiety is spreading into daytime functioning, not just bedtime
- sleep remains stuck after 1-2 weeks of a calm, consistent response
