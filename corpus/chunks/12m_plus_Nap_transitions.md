# Nap Transitions: 12 Months and Beyond

As your child moves past their first birthday and into toddlerhood and preschool years, their sleep needs continue to change, most notably with nap transitions from two naps to one, and eventually, dropping daytime naps altogether. These shifts are a natural part of development, reflecting their increasing wakefulness and ability to consolidate sleep.

Around 12 months, many toddlers are still having two naps a day. However, between 12 months and 3 years, most children will transition from two naps to just one. You might notice the morning nap becoming longer and later, while the afternoon nap gradually shortens and eventually disappears. By 3 years, about a quarter of children have stopped napping, and by 5 years, most children no longer need daytime naps if they are getting enough sleep at night.

Toddlers typically need 11-14 hours of sleep over 24 hours, usually consisting of 10-12 hours at night and a single nap of 1-2 hours during the day. Preschoolers (3-5 years) need 10-13 hours of night sleep, and some may still have a short nap of about an hour.

## Key Points
- The transition from two naps to one typically occurs between 12 months and 3 years.
- Most children stop napping altogether by 5 years if night sleep is sufficient.
- Toddlers usually need 10-12 hours of night sleep and one 1-2 hour nap.
- Preschoolers may have a 1-hour nap or no naps, needing 10-13 hours of night sleep.

## What to Try
- **Observe for readiness:** Signs your child might be ready to drop a nap include consistently resisting a nap, taking a long time to fall asleep for a nap, or having difficulty falling asleep at bedtime.
- **Gradually adjust nap times:** If transitioning from two naps to one, try pushing the morning nap later and shortening the afternoon nap until they merge into a single midday nap.
- **Maintain a consistent bedtime:** A regular bedtime, even on weekends, helps regulate their internal body clock and can improve night sleep, which is crucial when dropping naps.
- **Create a calming pre-sleep routine:** For both naps and bedtime, a predictable routine (e.g., quiet play, reading a book, cuddles) helps signal that it's time to wind down.
- **Offer quiet rest time:** If your child stops napping but still seems tired, encourage a period of quiet rest or independent play in their room.
- **Ensure an early bedtime:** If your child is dropping a nap, an earlier bedtime might be necessary to prevent overtiredness and ensure they get enough total sleep.

## When to Seek Help
- If your child's nap transitions are causing significant distress, frequent night wakings, or persistent grumpiness and tiredness during the day.
- If you are concerned about your child's overall sleep duration or quality, or if they are showing signs of a sleep problem like snoring or sleep apnoea.
- If you are struggling to manage the changes or feel overwhelmed. Your child and family health nurse or GP can provide tailored strategies and support.
