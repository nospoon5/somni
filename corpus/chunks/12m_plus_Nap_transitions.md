# Nap Transitions After 12 Months

After the first birthday, the big nap question is usually not "Should all toddlers be on one nap now?" It is "Is this child actually ready, or are we about to create an overtired disaster by pushing too hard too soon?"

Many 12-14 month olds are in the messy middle: too old for an easy textbook two-nap day, but not actually ready to cold-turkey to one nap every day. That is where bridging matters.

## What Readiness Actually Looks Like

### Probably not ready yet

- the morning nap still lands easily most days
- bedtime falls apart after a one-nap day
- the midday nap is too short to carry the whole afternoon
- early waking or split nights show up when you push the first nap too late

### Closer to ready

- one of the two naps is being refused most days for 1-2 weeks
- the second nap is drifting so late that bedtime is getting pushed out
- your toddler can comfortably stay awake much longer in the morning without melting down
- the midday nap has become the stronger, more restorative nap

At 13 months, it is very common to need a bridge period rather than a clean overnight switch.

## Key Points
- The 2 to 1 nap transition often starts after 12 months, but readiness varies a lot.
- A toddler can look "in between" for weeks without that meaning the schedule is wrong.
- Bridging is often better than forcing a cold-turkey switch.
- Early bedtime is part of the transition plan, not a sign of failure.

## What to Try
- **Use a short morning bridge if needed:** A 10-15 minute car, pram, or contact doze can take the edge off without replacing the main midday nap.
- **Cap the first nap if it is stealing from the second:** This helps if the real problem is that nap two is drifting too late.
- **Alternate between one-nap and two-nap days when needed:** This is normal in the messy middle and often works better than forcing one pattern every day.
- **Protect bedtime:** If the nap transition created a stretched afternoon, bring bedtime earlier instead of pushing through.
- **Move the main nap gradually, not in one huge jump:** Large schedule jumps usually create overtiredness faster than readiness.

## When to Seek Help
- If the transition is causing persistent distress, repeated split nights, or clear chronic overtiredness.
- If your toddler is sleeping very little across 24 hours and not coping well in the day.
- If you are unsure whether the issue is readiness, schedule, or an unrelated sleep problem, your child and family health nurse or GP can help you sort that out.
