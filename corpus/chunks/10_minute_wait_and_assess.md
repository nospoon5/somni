# The 10-Minute Wait and Assess Rule

The 10-minute wait is not about leaving a baby to fend for themselves and it is definitely not a "cry it out" instruction. It is a short pause that gives a baby space to practise settling, work through a small wobble, and show you whether they can resettle with a bit of room.

The idea is simple: if your baby is safe, fed, dry, and in a suitable sleep space, you can pause before rushing in every time they grizzle or cry. Sometimes babies are in the middle of a sleep transition and only need a tiny bit of time to link things back together. Other times, the crying quickly escalates and that is your cue to step in sooner.

The key is to treat the wait as an assessment, not a rule you push through no matter what. If your baby sounds more upset by the minute, if the cry changes in a way that worries you, or if something about the situation feels off, go in and respond. The wait should always sit inside a responsive, loving approach.

## Key Points
- The pause is about giving space to practise self-settling, not ignoring distress.
- A baby can sometimes resettle if given a brief chance to bridge a sleep cycle.
- Escalating crying means you should respond, not keep waiting blindly.
- This approach only makes sense when the baby is safe, well, and otherwise ready for sleep.

## What to Try
- Start the timer only after you have ruled out obvious needs like hunger, discomfort, or a dirty nappy.
- Watch the pattern of the crying. If it softens, pauses, or changes into grizzling, they may be settling.
- If the crying ramps up, go in with calm reassurance and help them reset.
- Keep your response consistent so your baby learns that you are available, while still being given a small chance to practise.

## When to Seek Help
- If your baby is unusually hard to settle for long periods.
- If the crying feels like pain, illness, or something just does not seem right.
- If the settling approach is leaving you overwhelmed or unsure, pause and get support from your GP or child health nurse.
