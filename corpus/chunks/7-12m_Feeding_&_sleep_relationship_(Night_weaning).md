# Feeding and Sleep for Babies (7-12 Months)

As your baby moves through 7 to 12 months, their sleep patterns continue to evolve, with many having longer stretches of sleep at night. However, it's still quite normal for babies in this age group to wake at least once during the night. These wakings can be for comfort, due to developmental milestones, or sometimes still for a feed.

While solids are introduced around 6 months, milk feeds remain a primary source of nutrition. Around 9 months, the order might shift from milk first then solids, to solids first then milk. It's important to follow your baby's hunger cues for both. There's no evidence that solids will make your baby sleep longer.

If you're comfortable with night feeding, there's no rush to stop. However, if your baby is healthy and growing well, you can consider night weaning for formula-fed babies from 6 months, and for breastfed babies from 12 months. Before 12 months, night weaning breastfed babies can impact your milk supply.

## Key Points
- Night waking is still a normal part of development for babies aged 7-12 months.
- Solids are an important part of their diet, but milk feeds remain crucial.
- Night weaning for breastfed babies is generally recommended from 12 months, while for formula-fed babies it can be considered from 6 months.
- Feeding to sleep is a natural comfort for many babies and not a "bad habit."

## What to Try
- **Integrate solids thoughtfully:** Offer solids as part of their daytime routine, but continue to offer milk feeds first (or after solids from 9 months) to ensure adequate nutrition.
- **Phase out night feeds gradually:** If you choose to night wean, do so gently. For breastfed babies, gradually reduce feeding time by 2-5 minutes every second night. For formula-fed babies, reduce the volume by 20-30ml every second night.
- **Offer comfort for non-hunger wakings:** If your baby wakes and you suspect it's not due to hunger, try responsive settling techniques like gentle patting, shushing, or quiet words to help them resettle.
- **Ensure adequate daytime feeds:** Make sure your baby is getting enough milk and solids during the day to reduce the likelihood of genuine hunger at night.
- **Maintain a consistent bedtime routine:** A calming routine helps signal that it's time for sleep, which can support easier settling.

## When to Seek Help
- If your baby is not gaining weight as expected, or you have concerns about their overall nutrition.
- If your baby's sleep patterns suddenly change significantly and are accompanied by distress, illness, or a noticeable change in behaviour.
- If you are struggling with the decision to night wean, or finding the process challenging and stressful.
- If you are feeling overwhelmed, exhausted, or experiencing symptoms of low mood or anxiety; your GP or child and family health nurse can offer valuable support.
