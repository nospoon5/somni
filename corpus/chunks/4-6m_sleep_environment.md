# Optimising Your Baby's Sleep Environment (4-6 Months)

As your baby grows from 4 to 6 months, their sleep patterns become more regular, and they start to develop important motor skills like rolling. Adjusting their sleep environment to support these changes, particularly regarding clothing, is essential for their comfort and safety.

## Key Points
- Maintaining clear day-night light cues helps consolidate sleep.
- A consistent, calm sound environment continues to be beneficial.
- Transitioning from wrapping is crucial as your baby learns to roll.

## What to Try
- **Light:** Continue to use light as a signal for day and night. Keep your baby's sleep space dim or dark for naps and overnight sleep to encourage longer stretches. During awake times, expose them to natural daylight to reinforce their circadian rhythm. If you need a night-light for checks or feeds, choose a dim one that won't overstimulate them.
- **Noise:** A quiet and consistent noise level is generally best for sleep. If your baby finds white noise or soft music soothing, continue to use it at a low volume, ensuring the device is not too close to their ears. This can help block out sudden household noises that might otherwise wake them.
- **Clothing:** This age marks a significant transition for clothing, especially with wrapping. As soon as your baby shows the first signs of rolling (which often happens around 3-4 months), it's important to stop wrapping or swaddling them. They need their arms free to push up and reposition themselves if they roll onto their tummy. Instead, opt for a safe infant sleeping bag with fitted neck and armholes and no hood. Alternatively, use lightweight blankets tucked in firmly at chest level, ensuring your baby's feet are positioned at the bottom of the cot to prevent them from wriggling down and covering their face. Continue to dress your baby in layers, similar to your own comfort, and always keep their head uncovered to prevent overheating.

## When to Seek Help
- If your baby is consistently struggling to settle or sleep, or if you have concerns about their comfort or safety in their sleep environment, reach out to your child and family health nurse or GP.
- If your baby seems unwell, is difficult to rouse, or you notice any changes in their breathing patterns, seek medical advice promptly.
