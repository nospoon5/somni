# Newborn Nap Schedules and Wake Windows (0-3 Months)

Welcome to the wonderful, often unpredictable, world of newborn sleep! In these early months, your baby is just beginning to understand day from night, and their sleep patterns are still developing. It's a time for flexibility and responsiveness, focusing on your baby's unique cues.

Newborns typically sleep a lot, usually between 14 to 17 hours over a 24-hour period, though some may sleep as little as 11 or as much as 18 hours. This sleep is often broken into short bursts, usually lasting 2 to 4 hours at a time, spread across both day and night. Their tiny tummies mean they need frequent feeds, which naturally leads to waking often.

Your baby's sleep cycles are quite short, around 40 to 50 minutes, moving between active (dreaming) and quiet sleep. They can be easily roused during active sleep. Wake windows – the time your baby is happily awake between sleeps – are generally brief, often between 1 to 2 hours for newborns up to 6 weeks, and extending slightly to 1 to 2.5 hours for babies aged 6 to 12 weeks. Observing your baby's tired signs is key to knowing when it's time for another rest.

## Key Points
- Newborns sleep 14-17 hours in 24 hours, in short bursts of 2-4 hours.
- Day-night patterns are still developing and emerge around 4 weeks.
- Wake windows are typically 1-2 hours (0-6 weeks) and 1-2.5 hours (6-12 weeks).
- Babies need frequent feeds, often every 2-4 hours, which influences waking.
- Newborn sleep cycles are short (40-50 minutes) and include active and quiet sleep.

## What to Try
- **Follow a flexible "feed, play, sleep" pattern:** Offer a feed when your baby wakes, engage in a short period of gentle play or cuddles, then put them down for sleep when you notice tired signs.
- **Emphasise day and night:** Keep daytime bright and active, and night-time dim and quiet, even during feeds, to help establish their circadian rhythm.
- **Respond to tired signs promptly:** Look for cues like yawning, frowning, jerky movements, or rubbing eyes. Settling your baby before they become overtired can make it easier for them to fall asleep.
- **Use responsive settling techniques:** Gentle methods like holding, rocking, patting, or soft singing can help your baby feel secure and calm as they drift off to sleep. You can't spoil a newborn with comfort.
- **Consider wrapping:** Lightweight cotton or muslin wraps can help some babies feel secure and reduce the startle reflex, aiding sleep. Ensure arms are above waist level and legs can bend at the hips. Discontinue wrapping when your baby shows signs of rolling.

## When to Seek Help
- If your baby is consistently very sleepy, hard to wake, or isn't feeding enough (at least 8 times in 24 hours).
- If you have concerns about your baby's weight gain or overall health.
- If you're feeling overwhelmed or struggling to cope with broken sleep. Your child health nurse or GP can offer support and guidance.
